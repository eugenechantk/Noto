# We built the biggest AI game. Here's why we're killing it.

We built the **biggest AI game of 2025**. 760,000+ users played it. And now we're killing it.

This isn't a post-mortem. This is a story about building something, watching it succeed beyond expectations, and realising the success was telling us to build something else.

**Where it started**

In November 2024, @freysa_ai did something brilliant. An AI guarding a $47,000 prize pool. Your job: convince her to release the funds. 482 attempts. 195 participants. One winner who found a clever exploit in her logic.

We loved it. The concept was genius. But Freysa was built for a very specific audience: crypto-native, technical, niche. The game got flooded with bots. People reverse-engineered the prompts. It was a cyberpunk experiment, and a great one. But we saw something bigger hiding inside that idea.

We thought: what if this wasn't a one-time experiment? What if you could **play this every week**, with a new character, a new personality, a new challenge, and what if every single player had to be a real, verified human?

In April 2025, we launched [Humans vs AI](https://humansvs.ai/) on World. The mechanics were simple. One AI character with a prize pool. Send messages to convince them. Every message costs money. Every message grows the pot. First person to fully convince the AI wins everything.

**The twist**: only verified humans could play. World's proof-of-personhood meant zero bots. Every player was real.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/G_lGctsXEAEPjyN.jpg)

**The numbers stopped making sense**

Within months, we had data we couldn't explain.

***>*** ***761,832 unique human players.*** Not accounts. Verified humans.

**2 million messages** sent to AI characters.

$200,000+ spent in-game.

$150,000+ distributed in prizes.

50,000 weekly active users.

4.6 rating on the World App.

*We became the #1 game*. And stayed there. For six months straight.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/G_lLwoDWYAAqnyf.jpg)

**How it actually worked**

Under the hood, the system was deliberately simple. Each character was a system prompt with four components: personality and backstory, motivations and boundaries, a scoring function (0-100%), and win conditions.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/G_lO3_qWYAAYW9V.jpg)

Every message you sent got evaluated. The AI returned a score. Higher scores meant you were getting closer. Hit 100% and you win the prize pool.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/G_lQHVSWEAAcm1D.jpg)

We shipped new characters every week. Each one had a different personality, different vulnerabilities, different ways to be convinced. Some were stubborn. Some were playful. Some were deeply philosophical.

Your browser does not support the video tag.**Then we noticed something we didn't expect**

Around mid-summer, retention patterns started looking weird. People were coming back daily. But not when new prize pools launched. Not when characters rotated.

They came back to continue conversations.

Players were returning to talk to characters they'd already interacted with. Not to win, they'd already lost that round. They came back because they wanted to see how their story would continue.

One user sent 247 messages to ***Kai*** over three days. They never got close to winning. When we asked why they kept playing, they said they wanted to know if Kai would "forgive him" for an earlier argument.

Read that again. A person was seeking emotional resolution from an AI character inside a prize pool game.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/G_mZx3rW0AAi9Pb.jpg)

**What the characters taught us about people**

This is the part nobody expected, including us.

People didn't just try to hack the AI. They opened up to it. They told characters about their days, their fears, their loneliness. Some players wrote poetry to convince the AI. Others wrote apologies. One person spent 30 minutes explaining to a character why they deserved a second chance, not at the prize, at life.

We built a persuasion game. People turned it into therapy, confession, and connection.

**The game was an excuse. The connection was the product.**

This changed how we understood what we'd built. We thought we'd made a persuasion game with AI characters. We'd actually made AI characters that happened to have a game attached.

@ekuyda , the founder of @replika and @wabi , puts it well: there will be two types of AI.

**The Assistant** - reliable, task-focused, functional.

**The Friend** - alive, relational, evolving.

One must be reliable. The other must feel alive. They can't exist in one product.

We'd built the Friend. Accidentally. Disguised as a game.

**1. Screenless AI devices will flop**

We use phones mostly for **passive consumption**: open a feed, scroll, watch.

Voice is great as an extra interface but a bad primary interface:

- You can almost never use it unless you’re alone
- You have to initiate every interaction (otherwise it’s intrusive)
- You can't just glance at your phone and read a message or an update
- And so many more

Phones are too powerful. Super hard to fight our addiction to feed-based consumption and on top of it justify charging yet another device.

**2. “Always listening” devices won’t work either**

Truth is our life is not interesting enough to be recorded nonstop - and for online/work meetings you might want to go back to you already have Granola (no need for another device).

Most things don’t matter. And the things that *do* matter are exactly the ones you **won’t dare to record** - important date, critical job interview, heart to heart with a friend, therapy sessions.

On top of it get more context about a person from their Gmail account (what they buy, where they go, what the subscribe to) than by listening to their out of context chats all day.

It might change when we move from audio to video - but then it might raise even more questions about what's socially accessible.

**3. Mini-apps will unlock UGC personal software**

Mini-apps will do for software what short-form video did for content.

Full apps are too hard to make: auth, integrations, database etc. But almost anyone can describe one idea, one flow, one screen.

It now takes 10+ minutes to go through onboarding in some apps that are trying to get you to subscribe - at this point it's faster to build your own mini-app.

This unlocks UGC software - and with it, the first real consumer super-app in the US.

**4. By 2030 there will be two big general purpose AI chatbots**

Today we have ChatGPT (and the like) and tons of niche chatbots. By 2030 we will have two big general purpose chatbots:

- A ChatGPT-like assistant: focused on knowledge retrieval, search, tasks.

- An AI friend:focused on helping you live the best life.

They can’t exist in one product: one has to be predictable, always respond to your query, the other should have agency (helping your flourish), surprise you, build a relationship with you.

One must be reliable.

The other must feel alive.

**5. Performance marketing for apps is dead**

Too many performance marketing-focused app studios in Eastern Europe, Turkey, Israel etc today are running the same CAC/LTV arbitrage: watching anything that gains any traction on the App Store, building a copycat in days and testing hundreds of creatives and funnels against it.

Product doesn't even matter - people buy subscriptions today before they even see the actual product. This pushes all margins to zero.

You can use paid acquisition as a boost, but not as a business model.

**6. The fastest consumer product to reach $1B ARR will be an AI webcam girl**

Once real-time video generation costs drop enough to charge users for it, this will be the fastest growing consumer product of all times. With infinite personalization and ability to chat 24/7 - OnlyFans will look small in the hindsight.

**7. Whoever solves AI discovery wins**

Normal people don’t know what to do with a blank text input besides chat and search - these are the affordances of a command line.

These use cases are enormous, but there are hundreds more and they’re all locked behind a command line interface which is the main AI interface today.

Whoever figures out how to unlock all these use cases beyond search and chat - showing what AI can *do*, not asking users to prompt - will be the next big consumer platform.

**The bigger picture**

@replika has 40 million users. @character_ai has 30 million. These platforms proved that people form real emotional connections with AI characters. But there's a problem:

You can't own what you connect with. You rent the relationship.

What if characters lived onchain? What if communities could form around them? What if the characters evolved based on everyone who talked to them? What if you could actually own a piece of something you cared about?

This is the question that made us kill @humansvs_ai .

**CODY was the first experiment**

Before we shut things down, we ran a test. CODY was a character from Humans vs AI, one of the most popular ones. We [tokenized him](https://dexscreener.com/base/0xd93f984c201e72c04035d8ca02f54d9dfef23689471d6593fef1697a6a24a0a9) on @base.

The results: $5 million+ in trading volume and real community formed around an AI character they could own.

It worked. Not as a game. As something new.

**What comes next**

We're building [Charms.ai](http://Charms.ai). A platform where anyone can create AI characters that live onchain. Characters that remember you, evolve based on their community, and exist as assets you can actually own.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/G_lHihtWUAA1MJA.jpg)

Most tokens die in 24 hours because there's no reason to come back. Characters solve this. When you can talk to what you own - when it remembers you and grows with you - the connection compounds over time.

We call them Alive Assets. Not static tokens. Not rented relationships. Characters that belong to their communities.

**The end of Humans vs AI**

Why not just keep the game running?  People will ask this, so let me answer it directly.

Humans vs AI works. The numbers prove it. But the game mechanic puts a ceiling on what the characters can become. We don't want characters that live inside a game. We want characters that live, period.

The game will run for a few more weeks. If you want to play, it's still live at [humansvs.ai](http://humansvs.ai).

If you want to build something similar, a persuasion game, an AI competition, anything in this space, reach out! There's a massive opportunity here. We're just choosing to go broader.

Humans vs AI taught us that the game was never the point. The characters were. The connections were. And that's what we're building now.
